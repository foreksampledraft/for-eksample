Cloud Capital – the force behind technofeudalism

Yanis Varoufakis

"Serious power comes from serious wealth."

Adapted from the article's opening discussion of Batman and Justice League.

In Justice League, a Hollywood blockbuster that brought together a swath of superheroes in a bid to save Earth from desertification, there is a scene when Aquaman enters Bruce Wayne's car. The Batman example serves as an entry into the question of how wealth transforms into power. “What’s your superpowers again?” he asks with the impertinence of a superhero brat. “I am rich”, replies Wayne. Perhaps unintentionally, the screenwriters were making a fairly sophisticated point: serious power comes from serious wealth.

Commanding Capital

Nothing new there, you will remark. We have heard it all before—even from Abba singing “It’s a rich man’s world”. But what precisely is it that turns riches into a superpower?

Imagine wandering lost in the Sahara Desert on the verge of dying of thirst. Varoufakis introduces asymmetrical access to scarce resources as a primordial form of power. I approach you on a camel laden with water. Suddenly, I possess the power to make you "volunteer" to do things on my behalf.

From Don to Alexa

Varoufakis contrasts twentieth-century advertising with contemporary algorithmic systems. Don Draper from Mad Men functions as a metaphor for classical advertising logic. Don Draper sold desire. Alexa does something different. It continuously observes, predicts and reshapes behaviour through feedback loops.

Cloud-Proles

Cloud capital extends command into the workplace. Warehouse workers, drivers, and delivery workers find themselves increasingly managed by algorithms.

Cloud-Serfs

Perhaps the strongest argument in the essay is that the reproduction of cloud capital depends on unpaid labour. Uploading photographs. Posting comments. Writing reviews. Generating movement data. All of these actions reproduce the system itself. Unlike industrial capital, cloud capital relies on continuous unpaid participation by users.

Cloud-Fiefs

Varoufakis ultimately argues that platforms such as Amazon resemble feudal arrangements more than classical markets. Instead of a market populated by independent actors, platforms increasingly become controlled territories mediated by algorithms.

Editorial Note

This essay is an extract from the third chapter of Technofeudalism: What Killed Capitalism? (2023).

Source: Yanis Varoufakis, Technofeudalism: What Killed Capitalism?, The Bodley Head / Penguin Random House, 2023.
